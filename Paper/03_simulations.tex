\subsection{Centrality Analysis — Higgs Twitter}

Figure~\ref{fig:mention_centrality} (Section~\ref{sec:model}) shows the
top-15 nodes for the Mention network.
A consistent pattern emerges across all three Higgs networks: a single
dominant hub (node \texttt{88}) captures the highest in-degree and
eigenvector centrality, while a structurally distinct bridge node
(node \texttt{64911} in Retweet, \texttt{13808} in Reply) leads in
betweenness centrality.
This \textit{hub--bridge duality} suggests that the most-mentioned accounts
are not necessarily the primary conduits through which information flows
between communities.

Figure~\ref{fig:retweet_centrality} provides the equivalent four-panel view
for the Retweet network, which is the largest and most influential propagation
channel during the Higgs boson event.

\begin{figure*}[!t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/higgs_retweet_centrality.png}
    \caption{Top-15 nodes by each centrality metric in the Higgs Retweet network.
             Node \texttt{88} dominates in-degree (14{,}060 retweets) while
             \texttt{64911} leads in betweenness, confirming the hub--bridge
             distinction across all Higgs interaction types.}
    \label{fig:retweet_centrality}
\end{figure*}

\subsection{Top-10\% Metric Intersections}

Figure~\ref{fig:intersections} shows pairwise top-10\% intersections for
each network.
Several observations stand out:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{In-Deg $\cap$ Out-Deg} is the smallest intersection in all
          three networks, confirming that popular recipients generally do not
          also broadcast heavily — passive popularity and active participation
          are distinct roles.
    \item In the Retweet network, \textbf{Betweenness $\cap$ Eigenvector}
          yields 18{,}628 nodes — the largest pairwise intersection of any
          metric pair across all datasets — indicating that structural bridges
          and influential neighbors are strongly correlated in the retweet graph.
    \item The Reply network shows the tightest intersections overall,
          consistent with its smaller size and more conversational structure.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure*}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{figures/higgs_all_intersections.png}
    \caption{Top-10\% pairwise metric intersections for the Mention (top),
             Reply (middle), and Retweet (bottom) networks.
             In-Deg $\cap$ Out-Deg is consistently the smallest intersection,
             while Betweenness $\cap$ Eigenvector is the largest in the
             Retweet network.}
    \label{fig:intersections}
\end{figure*}

\subsection{Enron Email Network}

Figure~\ref{fig:enron_centrality} shows the top-15 Enron nodes by each
centrality metric.
\texttt{sally.beck@enron.com} (COO) dominates both in-degree and betweenness,
indicating she was both a primary recipient and a critical communication bridge
across departments.
High out-degree belongs to \texttt{sally.beck} and \texttt{bob.ambrecht},
consistent with executive broadcast behaviour.
Notably, betweenness and eigenvector centrality surface different executives
than in-degree alone — \texttt{don.baughman} ranks highly in betweenness
despite modest in-degree, suggesting a broker role spanning otherwise
disconnected groups.

\subsection{Facebook Ego-Network}

Figure~\ref{fig:fb_deg} shows the degree distribution and Louvain community
structure of the Facebook network.
The 16 communities range from 19 to 548 nodes and likely correspond to the
distinct social circles of the ten anonymised ego users; the uneven size
distribution reflects the varying social reach of each ego.
In contrast to Enron's 252 fine-grained departmental clusters, Facebook yields
far fewer but larger communities, consistent with the tighter clustering and
higher average degree (21.8) of a friendship graph.

\subsection{Random Graph Comparison}

To contextualise each network's structure, we compare it against three
canonical null models — Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi (ER)~\cite{erdos1959},
Watts--Strogatz (WS)~\cite{watts1998}, and Barab\'asi--Albert
(BA)~\cite{barabasi1999} — each instantiated with the same number of
nodes and matched average degree.
Comparisons are run on a 3{,}000-node subgraph sampled from the
largest connected component of each real network.

\begin{figure*}[!ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/random_graph_comparison.png}
    \caption{Clustering coefficient and average path length for each
             real network (solid bars) versus ER, WS, and BA null models
             (hatched bars). No single generative model matches both
             properties of any real network.}
    \label{fig:random_graph}
\end{figure*}

\begin{figure*}[!ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/random_graph_deg_comparison.png}
    \caption{Degree distributions of real networks (blue) versus BA
             (orange), WS (green), and ER (red) null models.
             The heavy-tailed behaviour of real networks is captured
             only by the BA model.}
    \label{fig:random_deg}
\end{figure*}

The key findings are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ER} replicates neither the clustering nor the
          degree heterogeneity of any real network.
    \item \textbf{WS} closely matches the clustering of Facebook
          ($\bar{C} = 0.606$) but underestimates clustering in
          the Higgs and Enron networks and produces Poisson-like
          degree distributions, not power-law tails.
    \item \textbf{BA} captures the heavy-tailed degree distributions
          of all five networks but produces near-zero clustering,
          diverging sharply from the real graphs — especially Facebook.
\end{itemize}
These results confirm that real-world networks are \textit{hybrid}
structures: their degree heterogeneity is BA-like while their local
density is WS-like, a combination no single generative model reproduces.

\subsection{Community Detection Comparison}

We compare Louvain~\cite{blondel2008} and Label Propagation on three
representative networks: Higgs Reply (directed, sparse), Enron (directed,
hierarchical), and Facebook (undirected, dense).
Table~\ref{tab:community_compare} reports the number of communities
detected and the modularity $Q$ achieved by each algorithm.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Louvain vs.\ Label Propagation: community count and modularity
         $Q$ on three networks.}
\label{tab:community_compare}
\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\toprule
 & \multicolumn{2}{c}{\textbf{Louvain}} & \multicolumn{2}{c}{\textbf{Label Prop.}} \\
\cmidrule(lr){2-3}\cmidrule(lr){4-5}
\textbf{Network} & \# Comm. & $Q$ & \# Comm. & $Q$ \\
\midrule
Higgs Reply  & 10{,}704 & 0.964 & 13{,}638 & 0.850 \\
Enron Email  &     254  & 0.628 &     465  & 0.382 \\
Facebook     &      16  & 0.835 &      44  & 0.737 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/community_comparison.png}
    \caption{Community count (left axis, bars) and modularity $Q$
             (right axis, markers) for Louvain (blue) and Label
             Propagation (orange) on the three test networks.
             Louvain achieves higher $Q$ in every case while
             producing fewer, semantically cleaner partitions.}
    \label{fig:community_compare}
\end{figure}

Louvain consistently achieves higher modularity than Label Propagation
across all three networks ($\Delta Q = 0.11$--$0.25$).
Label Propagation over-fragments by $1.3\times$ on Facebook (44 vs.\ 16
communities) and $1.8\times$ on Enron (465 vs.\ 254), producing
partitions that are harder to interpret semantically.
The Higgs Reply result is notable: both algorithms detect thousands of
micro-communities, reflecting the isolated thread structure of a
reply graph, but Louvain's higher $Q$ ($0.964$) indicates
better-defined community boundaries even at that granularity.
