\subsection{Centrality Analysis — Higgs Twitter}

Figure~\ref{fig:mention_centrality} (Section~\ref{sec:model}) shows the
top-15 nodes for the Mention network.
A consistent pattern emerges across all three Higgs networks: a single
dominant hub (node \texttt{88}) captures the highest in-degree and
eigenvector centrality, while a structurally distinct bridge node
(node \texttt{64911} in Retweet, \texttt{13808} in Reply) leads in
betweenness centrality.
This \textit{hub--bridge duality} suggests that the most-mentioned accounts
are not necessarily the primary conduits through which information flows
between communities.

Figure~\ref{fig:retweet_centrality} provides the equivalent four-panel view
for the Retweet network, which is the largest and most influential propagation
channel during the Higgs boson event.

\subsection{Top-10\% Metric Intersections}

Figure~\ref{fig:intersections} shows pairwise top-10\% intersections for
each network.
Several observations stand out:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{In-Deg $\cap$ Out-Deg} is the smallest intersection in all
          three networks, confirming that popular recipients generally do not
          also broadcast heavily — passive popularity and active participation
          are distinct roles.
    \item In the Retweet network, \textbf{Betweenness $\cap$ Eigenvector}
          yields 18{,}628 nodes — the largest pairwise intersection of any
          metric pair across all datasets — indicating that structural bridges
          and influential neighbors are strongly correlated in the retweet graph.
    \item The Reply network shows the tightest intersections overall,
          consistent with its smaller size and more conversational structure.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Enron Email Network}

Figure~\ref{fig:enron_centrality} shows the top-15 Enron nodes by each
centrality metric.
Unlike the anonymised Higgs and Facebook datasets, Enron email addresses can
be mapped to known corporate roles, enabling direct organisational interpretation.

\texttt{sally.beck@enron.com} — Enron's Chief Operating Officer — dominates
all four centrality panels: highest in-degree (539), highest out-degree
(1{,}245), highest betweenness ($1.1 \times 10^{7}$), and highest eigenvector
centrality (1.0 by normalisation).
This quadruple dominance is unique among all five networks studied and
represents a rare \textit{exception} to the hub--bridge duality observed
elsewhere.
In a centralised corporate hierarchy, the COO simultaneously receives the
most email, sends the most email, and bridges the most departments — roles
that are structurally separated in the flatter, broadcast-driven Higgs
networks.

The remaining top-ranked nodes map cleanly onto Enron's organisational
chart.
\texttt{kenneth.lay@enron.com} (Chairman and CEO) ranks second in
out-degree (915) — consistent with company-wide broadcast communication —
but only 35th in in-degree, suggesting his inbox was more heavily filtered
or delegated.
\texttt{rick.buy@enron.com} (Chief Risk Officer) ranks third in
in-degree (297) and second in eigenvector centrality (0.42), reflecting his
position as a convergence point for risk-related communication across
trading desks.
The eigenvector top-15 surfaces the senior trading leadership:
\texttt{john.lavorato@enron.com} (Head of North American Energy Trading,
eigenvector 0.30), \texttt{louise.kitchen@enron.com} (President of Enron
Online, 0.26), and \texttt{greg.whalley@enron.com} (President and later
acting CEO, 0.25) — all connected to other high-centrality nodes through
the trading division.

Betweenness centrality reveals a structurally distinct set of brokers.
\texttt{don.baughman@enron.com} ranks second in betweenness ($4.3 \times
10^{6}$) despite ranking only second in in-degree, indicating that his
communication bridged otherwise disconnected groups.
\texttt{larry.campbell@enron.com} ranks third in betweenness ($3.3 \times
10^{6}$) but does not appear in the eigenvector top-15, suggesting he
connected peripheral nodes rather than the executive core.
This pattern — betweenness surfacing middle-management brokers invisible
to degree or eigenvector measures — mirrors the hub--bridge duality seen
in the Higgs data, but at the level of organisational roles rather than
anonymous user IDs.

The Louvain community structure reinforces these observations.
Community~11 (1{,}103 nodes) contains \texttt{rick.buy}, \texttt{john.lavorato},
\texttt{louise.kitchen}, \texttt{greg.whalley}, and \texttt{richard.causey}
(Chief Accounting Officer) — essentially Enron's trading and financial
leadership.
Community~2 (1{,}801 nodes), the largest, is anchored by Sally Beck (COO)
alongside \texttt{wes.colwell}, \texttt{greg.piper}, and
\texttt{ted.murphy} — operations and infrastructure.
Community~37 (1{,}049 nodes) is headed by \texttt{kenneth.lay} with
a modest in-degree of just 35, consistent with a CEO community composed
primarily of external contacts and board-level correspondence rather than
day-to-day operational traffic.

\subsection{Graph Properties}

Table~\ref{tab:graph_props} summarises the clustering coefficient
$\bar{C}$ and average shortest-path length $\ell$ for all five networks,
computed on the largest weakly connected component.
Path lengths for the three large Higgs networks were estimated via BFS
sampling ($n{=}500$ source nodes); diameter values are lower bounds
from the same sampling procedure.

\begin{table}[!ht]
\centering
\caption{Clustering coefficient $\bar{C}$, sampled average path length
         $\ell$, and diameter lower bound $d$ for each network.}
\label{tab:graph_props}
\begin{tabular}{lrrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Network}  & $\bar{C}$ & $\ell$  & $d \geq$ \\
\midrule
Higgs Mention     & 0.087     & 5.08    & 17 \\
Higgs Reply       & 0.007     & 7.22    & 28 \\
Higgs Retweet     & 0.016     & 5.45    & 17 \\
Enron Email       & 0.174     & 4.21    & 11 \\
Facebook          & 0.606     & 3.72    &  8 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Three structural regimes emerge from Table~\ref{tab:graph_props}.
The \textbf{Facebook} network stands out with by far the highest clustering
coefficient ($\bar{C} = 0.606$) and the shortest average path length
($\ell \approx 3.7$) — the classic signature of a small-world
network~\cite{watts1998}.
Dense local triangles arise naturally in friendship graphs because mutual
friends tend to know one another, and the short paths reflect the
well-known ``six degrees of separation'' phenomenon compressed to under
four hops in a 4{,}039-node ego-network.

The \textbf{Enron} network occupies a middle ground ($\bar{C} = 0.174$,
$\ell \approx 4.2$), consistent with a hierarchically organised
communication graph.
Clustering is elevated relative to a random graph of equal size (ER
would predict $\bar{C} \approx 0.003$) because colleagues in the same
department email each other frequently, forming dense intra-departmental
triangles; the short path length reflects the small number of hops needed
to reach any employee through the executive hub structure.

The three \textbf{Higgs} networks exhibit qualitatively different
behaviour.
The Mention and Retweet networks have low clustering
($\bar{C} = 0.087$ and $0.016$) and moderate path lengths
($\ell \approx 5.1$ and $5.5$), consistent with sparse
broadcast topologies where a user mentions or retweets a popular
account without any reciprocal tie.
The \textbf{Reply network is a consistent outlier}: the lowest clustering
of any network studied ($\bar{C} = 0.007$), the longest average path
length ($\ell \approx 7.2$), and the largest diameter lower bound
($d \geq 28$).
This is a direct consequence of the \textit{tree-like topology} of
threaded conversations.
When user $A$ replies to user $B$ and user $C$ also replies to $B$,
the Reply graph contains edges $A \to B$ and $C \to B$ but typically
no edge $A \to C$ — suppressing triangle formation and driving
$\bar{C}$ toward zero.
Reply chains extend linearly ($A \to B \to D \to E \to \ldots$),
inflating path lengths and diameter far beyond what the Mention or
Retweet networks produce at comparable scale.
This structural difference explains why the Reply network also shows the
tightest top-10\% intersections (Section~\ref{sec:results}B) and why
both Louvain and Label Propagation detect thousands of micro-communities
in it: each isolated thread forms its own tiny cluster.

\subsection{Random Graph Comparison}

To contextualise each network's structure, we compare it against three
canonical null models — Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi (ER)~\cite{erdos1959},
Watts--Strogatz (WS)~\cite{watts1998}, and Barab\'asi--Albert
(BA)~\cite{barabasi1999} — each instantiated with the same number of
nodes and matched average degree.
Comparisons are run on a 3{,}000-node subgraph sampled from the
largest connected component of each real network.

\begin{figure*}[!ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/random_graph_comparison.png}
    \caption{Clustering coefficient and average path length for each
             real network (solid bars) versus ER, WS, and BA null models
             (hatched bars). No single generative model matches both
             properties of any real network.}
    \label{fig:random_graph}
\end{figure*}

\begin{figure*}[!ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/random_graph_deg_comparison.png}
    \caption{Degree distributions of real networks (blue) versus BA
             (orange), WS (green), and ER (red) null models.
             The heavy-tailed behaviour of real networks is captured
             only by the BA model.}
    \label{fig:random_deg}
\end{figure*}

The key findings are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ER} replicates neither the clustering nor the
          degree heterogeneity of any real network.
    \item \textbf{WS} closely matches the clustering of Facebook
          ($\bar{C} = 0.606$) but underestimates clustering in
          the Higgs and Enron networks and produces Poisson-like
          degree distributions, not power-law tails.
    \item \textbf{BA} captures the heavy-tailed degree distributions
          of all five networks but produces near-zero clustering,
          diverging sharply from the real graphs — especially Facebook.
\end{itemize}
Taken network-by-network, the closest generative model differs:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Facebook} is closest to \textbf{WS} in clustering
          ($\bar{C}_{\text{real}} = 0.606$ vs.\ $\bar{C}_{\text{WS}} \approx 0.53$)
          and closest to \textbf{BA} in degree distribution (heavy tail).
          This combination — dense local triangles \textit{plus} a few
          extremely high-degree hubs — is the signature of a friendship
          network where popular individuals accumulate connections
          preferentially while most social ties remain reciprocal and
          clustered.
    \item \textbf{Enron} is closest to \textbf{BA} in both degree
          heterogeneity and path length, but its clustering
          ($\bar{C} = 0.174$) exceeds both ER and BA by an order of
          magnitude.
          The residual clustering reflects departmental structure:
          colleagues within the same business unit email each other
          frequently, creating dense triangles that no preferential-attachment
          mechanism would produce.
    \item The \textbf{Higgs} networks have near-zero clustering across
          all three null models \textit{and} the real graphs
          ($\bar{C} < 0.09$), so clustering alone cannot discriminate
          models.
          Their degree distributions are unambiguously heavy-tailed (BA-like),
          and their short path lengths ($\ell \approx 5$--$7$) are
          consistent with all three models.
          These are \textit{broadcast networks}: information flows outward
          from hubs with minimal reciprocal interaction, so neither WS's
          local rewiring nor ER's uniform attachment is relevant.
\end{itemize}
In summary, real-world networks are \textit{hybrid} structures: their degree
heterogeneity is BA-like while their local density is WS-like, a combination
no single generative model reproduces.

Louvain consistently achieves higher modularity than Label Propagation
across all three networks ($\Delta Q = 0.11$--$0.25$).
Label Propagation over-fragments by $1.3\times$ on Facebook (44 vs.\ 16

The Higgs Reply result is notable: both algorithms detect thousands of
micro-communities, reflecting the isolated thread structure of a
reply graph, but Louvain's higher $Q$ ($0.964$) indicates
better-defined community boundaries even at that granularity.

\subsection{Synthesis: Three Structural Regimes}

The analyses above converge on a taxonomy of three distinct network types,
each characterised by a consistent bundle of structural properties.

\textbf{Broadcast networks} (Higgs Mention, Retweet, Reply) are sparse,
directed graphs dominated by a small number of hubs.
Their degree distributions are heavy-tailed (BA-like), clustering is
near-zero, and the hub--bridge duality is pronounced: the most-retweeted
account (node \texttt{88}) is not the primary betweenness bridge
(node \texttt{64911}).
Top-10\% intersections show that In-Deg $\cap$ Out-Deg is consistently
the smallest overlap, confirming that popularity and broadcasting are
distinct roles.
The Reply sub-network is an extreme case: its tree-like thread topology
produces the lowest clustering, longest paths, and most fragmented
community structure of any network in the study.

\textbf{Hierarchical networks} (Enron Email) have moderate clustering
reflecting departmental structure, short paths through executive hubs,
and a partial \textit{collapse} of the hub--bridge duality at the top
of the hierarchy.
Sally Beck (COO) simultaneously leads all four centrality rankings — a
pattern not seen in any other network — because a centralised corporate
structure concentrates both popularity and brokerage in the same executive
roles.
Below the C-suite, however, the duality re-emerges: middle-management
brokers like \texttt{don.baughman} and \texttt{larry.campbell} rank
highly in betweenness without appearing in the eigenvector top-15,
indicating that inter-departmental bridging and executive influence
remain structurally separate at lower levels of the org chart.

\textbf{Small-world networks} (Facebook) exhibit high clustering
($\bar{C} = 0.606$), short paths ($\ell \approx 3.7$), and well-separated
Louvain communities (16) that correspond to distinct social circles.
Comparison to null models shows that Facebook's clustering approaches
Watts--Strogatz while its degree distribution matches Barab\'asi--Albert,
confirming that friendship networks combine local reciprocity with
preferential attachment — a hybrid no single model captures.
Louvain's 16 communities align with the ten ego users' social circles
far better than Label Propagation's 44 over-fragmented partitions.

This three-way classification — broadcast, hierarchical, small-world —
provides a framework for predicting which centrality metrics and
community detection algorithms will be most informative for a given
network type.
In broadcast networks, betweenness reveals structurally important
brokers invisible to degree; in hierarchical networks, all metrics
converge on the same executives; in small-world networks, community
detection is the primary tool for uncovering latent group structure.
